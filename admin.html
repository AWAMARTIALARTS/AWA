Cardio Training Guide (Private)

All cardio work is prescribed by heart rate zone, not pace. Wear a heart rate monitor (chest strap or wrist) for every session. Zone 1 (50-60% Max HR) — Recovery. Very light effort, full conversation. Zone 2 (60-70% Max HR) — Aerobic Base. Comfortable, sustainable, slightly quickened breath. Zone 3 (70-80% Max HR) — Tempo. Moderately hard, breathing noticeably harder. Zone 4 (80-90% Max HR) — Threshold. Hard, short sentences only. Zone 5 (90-100% Max HR) — Max Effort. All-out, unsustainable beyond short bursts. Zone 2 Base Run: 30-45 min continuous, Zone 2, no rest. Long Zone 2 Conditioning: 45-60 min continuous, Zone 2, no rest. Tempo Intervals: 4 x 8 min, Zone 3, 3 min Zone 1 between reps. Threshold Repeats: 5 x 5 min, Zone 4, 2 min Zone 1 between reps. HIIT Sprints: 10 x 30 sec, Zone 5, 90 sec Zone 1 between reps. Estimating max HR: 220 minus age is a rough guide only — use a lab or field test where possible, and let how you feel override the numbers.

Strength Development Principles (Private)

Conjugate Method (Louie Simmons / Westside Barbell): rotate Max Effort work (heavy singles/doubles, 90%+, rotated every 1-2 weeks to avoid staleness) with Dynamic Effort work (50-60% loads moved explosively). Builds raw strength and the speed to express it. Triphasic Training (Cal Dietz): dedicate blocks to eccentric, then isometric, then concentric emphasis within the same lifts. Slow controlled eccentrics build tendon strength and force absorption — directly protective for a fighter landing kicks or checking shots. Base lifts: squat, hinge, press, and pull patterns cycled through intensity/volume waves as the foundation everything else is built on.

Power Development Principles (Private)

Rate of Force Development: lighter loads moved maximally fast — trap bar jumps, medicine ball throws, simplified Olympic lift derivatives — trains the nervous system to produce force quickly, not just a lot of it. Post-Activation Potentiation (contrast training): pair a heavy strength movement with an explosive movement of a similar pattern (e.g. heavy trap bar deadlift into broad jumps). Use sparingly, in-season, as a peaking tool — fight-week or block-ending sessions — not a daily staple.

Explosiveness Development Principles (Private)

Plyometric progression: start low-amplitude (pogo hops, ankle bounces) before progressing to high-amplitude (broad jumps, depth jumps). Technique before intensity, always. Reactive strength: minimise ground contact time in jumps and bounds to train the stretch-shortening cycle — the same mechanism behind fast strikes and quick footwork off the back foot.

Movement Development Principles (Private)

Joint-by-Joint Approach (Mike Boyle): each joint alternates between needing mobility or stability. Bias unilateral work — split squats, single-leg RDLs, lateral lunges — since striking and grappling both happen from a staggered, often single-leg base. Movement-Based Training (Vern Gambetta): multi-directional footwork drills, rotational power work (cable rotations, med ball throws), and reactive agility build the general athleticism that specific technique sits on top of. Work Capacity & Aerobic Base (Joel Jamieson): a bigger aerobic engine (built through Zone 2 conditioning above) speeds recovery between rounds and between explosive efforts within a round — power output stays higher for longer.

Mission Statement

Martial arts is complex, and everyone develops at their own pace. Patience is the foundation every level is built on — come ready to work, ready to be patient with yourself, and ready to trust the process. This is the private reference copy. The same message appears publicly on the homepage and Combat Fitness page, worded for visitors.

8-Week Combat Fitness Transformation — Coach Syllabus

Full syllabus reference: see the AWA Combat Fitness Transformation Manual (Word document) for the complete AWA Strike Level 1-5 breakdown, weekly schedule, and grading criteria. Quick reference: Level 1 — Foundations: guard, range, blocking the 1,2,3, straight-shot returns (1,2 / 1,2,1 / 1,1,2 / 2,1,2) Level 2 — Head Movement & Lead Hand: 1,3 / 1,1 / 3,1 / slip,3 — rear hand added once balance holds Level 3 — Mid-Range Fighting: 1,2,3 / 1,2,3,4 / 2,3,2 Level 4 — Range Transition: 1,1 / 1,1,2-move right / 3,2,roll / 2,3,2,roll / 1,slip 2,slip / 2,3 Level 5 — Full integration, pads at competition pace, final grading 8-week schedule: two levels introduced per fortnight, Week 8 reserved for Level 5 and grading.
